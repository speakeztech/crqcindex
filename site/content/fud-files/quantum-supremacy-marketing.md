## Overview

The term "quantum supremacy" has been systematically repurposed from its original narrow technical meaning — demonstrating a quantum computation infeasible for classical computers — into a marketing vehicle for investment narratives and press coverage.

## Why This Signal Is Excluded

The CRQC Index excludes supremacy claims from Z estimation for specific reasons:

1. **Supremacy demonstrations target artificially constrained problems** that do not map to cryptanalytic workloads
2. **The gap between supremacy and utility remains vast** — sampling from random circuits does not factor integers
3. **Press coverage amplifies without context**, creating the impression of imminent threat where none exists
4. **Vendor incentives skew reporting** — quantum computing companies have existential financial interest in overstating nearness to practical utility

## The Pattern

A typical cycle: vendor announces supremacy milestone → press reports "quantum computers can now..." → policy makers react to headlines → security budgets shift prematurely or in wrong directions → actual threat timeline remains unchanged.

## What to Watch Instead

The CRQC Index focuses on signals that directly reduce the resource requirements for running Shor's or Grover's algorithms at cryptographically relevant scales. Supremacy milestones become relevant only when the demonstrated computation has structural similarity to the target cryptanalytic workload.
